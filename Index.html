Know Your Breast, Know Your Health

Breast Self-Examination (BSE) Awareness Guide for Young Women

What is Breast Self-Examination (BSE)?

Breast Self-Examination (BSE) is a simple way of looking at and feeling your breasts regularly to check for any unusual changes. When you know how your breasts normally look and feel, it becomes easier to notice changes early.

BSE is only a self-awareness practice. BSE is about knowing your body better — not diagnosing a disease.

Most breast changes are not cancer, especially in teenagers. But knowing your normal makes it easier to notice when something is different.

Why Is BSE Important? (Even During Adolescence)

  • Builds body awareness
  • Promotes responsible health habits
  • Encourages early help-seeking behavior
  • Reduces fear through knowledge
  • Empowers young women to take care of their health

Who Can Perform BSE?

  • Girls and women who have started menstruating
  • Young women (including teenagers) to build awareness
  • Adult women of all ages

How to Perform Breast Self-Examination

1. LOOK (In Front of a Mirror)

  • Remove clothing above the waist
  • Stand in front of a mirror with arms at your sides
  • Observe for changes in size, shape, dimpling, redness, swelling, or nipple changes
  • Repeat with hands on hips, arms raised, and slight forward bending

2. FEEL (While Lying Down)

  • Lie on your back with a pillow under your shoulder
  • Place one arm behind your head
  • Use the flat part of your three middle fingers
  • Move fingers in circular motions using light, medium, and deep pressure
  • Follow an up-and-down pattern covering the entire breast and armpit
  • Repeat on the other breast

3. CHECK THE ARMPITS

  • Examine both armpits for lumps or thickening
  • Gently squeeze nipple for unusual discharge

When Should You Do BSE?

  • Once every month
  • Best time: 3–5 days after menstruation starts
  • If no regular period: choose the same day each month
Consistency helps you notice changes more easily.

What to Look or Feel For

  • New lump, mass, or thickening
  • Unexplained change in size or shape
  • Swelling on one side
  • Skin dimpling or puckering
  • Nipple turning inward
  • Breast or nipple pain

What To Do If You Notice Changes

  • Stay calm
  • Inform a parent or guardian
  • Consult a school nurse or doctor
Most breast lumps in teenagers are NOT cancer.

Healthy Habits for Breast Health

  • Balanced diet
  • Regular physical activity
  • Avoid smoking
  • Maintain healthy weight
